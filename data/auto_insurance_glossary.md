# Auto Insurance Glossary and Concept Guide

This reference defines common auto insurance concepts, coverage types, and terms.
Use it to answer general educational questions such as "what is X" and
"how does X work". It is not specific to any single customer or policy.

## Coverage Types

### Liability Coverage
Liability coverage pays for injuries and property damage you cause to other
people in an at-fault accident. It has two parts: Bodily Injury (BI) liability,
which covers other people's medical costs, and Property Damage (PD) liability,
which covers damage to other people's vehicles or property. Liability coverage
is required in most states and does not pay for your own vehicle or injuries.

### Collision Coverage
Collision coverage pays to repair or replace your vehicle when it is damaged in
a collision with another vehicle or object (for example a guardrail or tree),
regardless of fault. It is subject to a deductible. Collision is typically
optional unless required by a lender or lessor.

### Comprehensive Coverage
Comprehensive coverage (sometimes called "other than collision") pays for damage
to your vehicle from events that are not collisions: theft, vandalism, fire,
falling objects, hail, flood, storms, and animal strikes. It is subject to a
deductible and is usually optional unless required by a lender or lessor.

### Collision Damage Waiver (CDW)
A Collision Damage Waiver (CDW), also called a Loss Damage Waiver (LDW), is not
technically insurance. It is an optional add-on most often offered by car rental
companies that waives your financial responsibility for damage to, or theft of,
the rental vehicle. If you accept a CDW, the rental company agrees not to charge
you for covered damage to the rental car, typically subject to conditions and
exclusions. Personal auto policies with collision and comprehensive coverage may
already extend to rental cars, which can make a CDW redundant; check your policy
and credit card benefits before purchasing one.

### Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage (UM/UIM)
Uninsured Motorist (UM) coverage pays for your injuries (and in some states,
property damage) when you are hit by a driver who has no insurance.
Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage applies when the at-fault driver has
insurance, but not enough to cover your losses. UM/UIM helps protect you from
drivers without adequate coverage.

### Personal Injury Protection (PIP)
Personal Injury Protection, also known as "no-fault" coverage, pays for medical
expenses, and sometimes lost wages and other costs, for you and your passengers
regardless of who caused the accident. PIP is required in some states and
optional or unavailable in others.

### Medical Payments Coverage (MedPay)
Medical Payments coverage pays for reasonable medical and funeral expenses for
you and your passengers after an accident, regardless of fault. It is generally
a smaller limit than PIP and does not cover lost wages.

### Gap Insurance
Guaranteed Asset Protection (Gap) insurance pays the difference between what you
owe on a vehicle loan or lease and the vehicle's actual cash value if the car is
totaled or stolen. It is most useful when you owe more than the vehicle is worth.

### Roadside Assistance
Roadside Assistance is an optional coverage that pays for services such as
towing, jump-starts, flat-tire changes, lockout service, and fuel delivery when
your vehicle is disabled.

### Rental Reimbursement
Rental Reimbursement coverage pays, up to a daily and total limit, for a rental
car while your vehicle is being repaired after a covered claim.

## Key Terms

### Deductible
A deductible is the amount you agree to pay out of pocket on a covered claim
before your insurance pays the rest. For example, with a $500 deductible on a
$3,000 covered repair, you pay $500 and the insurer pays $2,500. Choosing a
higher deductible usually lowers your premium but increases your out-of-pocket
cost at claim time. Deductibles typically apply to collision and comprehensive
coverage.

### Premium
A premium is the amount you pay for your insurance policy, usually billed
monthly, semi-annually, or annually. Premiums are based on rating factors such
as the vehicles insured, drivers, location (garaging address), mileage, coverage
selections, deductibles, and claim history.

### Policy Limit
A policy limit is the maximum amount an insurer will pay for a covered loss.
Limits may be stated per person, per accident, or per coverage. Losses above the
limit are your responsibility.

### Actual Cash Value (ACV)
Actual Cash Value is the value of your vehicle at the time of loss, equal to its
replacement cost minus depreciation. Comprehensive and collision claims for a
totaled vehicle are typically settled at ACV.

### Total Loss
A vehicle is a total loss (or "totaled") when the cost to repair it, plus
salvage value, meets or exceeds the vehicle's actual cash value, or a
state-defined threshold. The insurer then pays the ACV instead of repairing it.

### Named Insured and Named Driver
The named insured is the person who holds the policy. A named driver (or listed
driver) is a person specifically listed on the policy who is permitted to drive
the insured vehicles and is considered in rating.

### SR-22
An SR-22 is not insurance; it is a certificate your insurer files with the state
to prove you carry at least the minimum required liability coverage. It is often
required after serious violations such as a DUI or driving without insurance.

### Telematics / Usage-Based Insurance
Telematics programs use a mobile app or plug-in device to measure driving
behavior such as mileage, braking, acceleration, and time of day. Safe driving
can earn discounts under a usage-based insurance program.

### Endorsement
An endorsement (or rider) is an amendment that adds, removes, or changes the
coverage on your policy, such as adding a new vehicle or driver, or adding
rental reimbursement.

## Common Questions

### What is the difference between collision and comprehensive?
Collision covers damage from hitting another vehicle or object. Comprehensive
covers non-collision events such as theft, vandalism, fire, weather, and animal
strikes. Both are typically optional and subject to a deductible.

### Do I need a CDW if I have full coverage?
If your personal auto policy includes collision and comprehensive coverage, it
often extends to rental vehicles, which may make a rental company's CDW
unnecessary. Some credit cards also provide rental damage benefits. Confirm with
your insurer and card issuer before declining or buying a CDW.

### How does a deductible affect my premium?
A higher deductible generally lowers your premium because you take on more of the
initial cost of a claim. A lower deductible raises your premium but reduces your
out-of-pocket cost when you file a claim.
